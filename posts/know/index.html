Notes on How We Know - Harry Binswanger

September 16, 2023

These are various notes on How We Know - Harry Binswanger. I focus on Chapters 7 and 10 since they are the most practical.

Chapter 7 - Logic: Practice

  • Logic covers concept formation, propositional judgment, and inference
  • Every word denotes a concept. The definition is a label on the concept.
  • Sensations don’t have definitions. Those things are. Foils and examples can explain it. Ex: existence, consciousness
  • Rules of proper definition
    • definition consists of genus and the differentia. No more extraneous elements
      • genus: the broader class containing the concept’s units and things from which it is different
      • differentia: distinguishes units from other examples in the genus
      • identifying genus’ is simple and immensely valuable conceptually.
        • ex: inflation - think of deflation and what is different between them. What’s the price level?
      • store concepts, a nested set of genera
      • definition is a proposition
    • definition must specify a group of referents in reality
      • failure modes
        • synonymy, circularity, vagueness, metaphor
    • definition must have the same scope as the concept
      • start at the definition from reality - have the units of the concept in mind, and then formulate the definition
    • definition must have the fundamental distinguishing characteristics
    • definition must be a single, economical sentence
  • Rand’s razor: do I need this concept?
    • counter-examples are “false division” or “false integration”
  • there is no inconsistency in perception, only in concepts.
  • truth is the recognition of reality - true prop corresponds to facts, truth is an awareness of the facts
  • logic is reality-based method of conceptual functioning
  • logic based on law of identity - and two corollaries non-contradiction and excluded middle.
  • logic is observation-based, non-contradictory and unit-economical

Chapter 10 - Free Will

  • perception is automatic, conceptual activities are volitional.
  • the function of the mind is not automatic
  • focus
    • thinking requires focused awareness. This is volitional
    • to focus is to set the goal of awareness
    • in focus is like steering a car. Two aspects: observing the road and steering wheel
    • directing your own confusion: “What’s confusing me?”
    • being in focus: make full awareness an absolute of mental functioning.
  • choice of action
    • perception is automatic and physiologically determined.
    • but awareness is conscious, deliberate, and introspectable
    • No effort is required to see words on a screen, but to understand what is written, integrate it, grasp implications, and judge validity - this is mental work.
    • “You have to will your brain to think.”
    • it is all or nothing - you are either surrendered to transient feelings or committed to full awareness
    • Being focused and rational leads to clearly allowing action to fall from desire.
    • You can choose between rational management of your mind and purposeless drift.
  • Volition and character
    • “‘Character’ means a man’s nature or identity insofar as this is shaped by the moral values he accepts and automatizes.”
    • character comes from your repeated choices
    • “Man is a being of self-made soul.”
    • Every choice leaves its trace.
  • Validation: This theory can only be validated from your own direct experience
  • “The unfocused life is not truly lived.”
